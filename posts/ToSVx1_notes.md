# Chapter 2:

States that the Physiocrats were the first ones to derive the proof that the alienation of labor and labour power creates surplus value, and identify specific material components where capital persists.

They however failed to consider the social/historical conditions that engendered the creation of specific components and unnecessarily restricted the study. These conditions may or may not be aligned with the general historical movement of the time: it makes/needs no proise.

Capital has various forms, and the Physiocrats aptly described capitalism as a process that fully understands labor power as being a tradable, sellable commodity, and that if the laborer were to only provide to the capitalists the exact amount of labor needed to pay off his sustenance, then there would exist no surplus value.

Physiocrats love agriculture and view it as the only environment where "surplus" can be created (for example, the average farmer consumes far less than what he produces in a year). In regular industry they argue that this relation is more elusive and thus harder to study due to the process of transaction as well as the influence from the global market.. debates about usury being a form of true surplus value or not (and they did correctly identify labor turned into surplus presupposes that this labor is productive labor).

### Contradictions with Physiocrats

This is foremost a feudalistic description of the relations of labor i.e. labor is tied to only the land and surplus value only appears as a "gift from nature". Not realistic.

Was the first system to describe capitalism as a system of exploitation over other's labor, but mistakenly the physiocrats incorrectly identified the exploited as analgous to the one doing the exploiting (farmer both owns and uses his means of work)

By mistifying capital as a gift from god... this assumption just then leads to the truth that any commodity can be simply factored down into quanta of agricultural labor time. Says nothing of note about the system as a whole. Turdot implicitly deduces that surplus value is begotten from unpaid labor, notes its physical value.

In the mercantile system surplus value is not compatible since whenever a person wins, another person loses. Thus no real surplus fluid in the entire system is generated, 1st law of thermodynamics in economics.

# Chapter 3:

### PT 1

Begins with A. Smith. He states that the wage of a worker (i.e. average/natural wages) must be at least the amount to sustain oneself. "The development of the productive powers do not benefit the laborer" -> Smith notes that if the development continued onwards in this current state, unmodified, the improvement of production would imply better conditions, but in reality the march towards the division of labor is inevitable

Smith makes mistakes whilst determining the value of a commodity. Instead of equating it with socially necessary labor time, he mistakes the values of labor as being a factor of the exchange values of labor (which are extremely volatile like any other exchange value of a commodity. )

A little thought experiment. Assume a worker can make commodities and also trade them for other commodities, and assume that LTV is true, in other words:

$$ M - C - M' \equiv C - C' $$

then say that we create a commodity and imbue it with 12 hours of labor time and trade a commodity with our commodity that also took 12 hour labor time. Then we have simply exchanged the same exact amount of use value, so no surplus value was generated, thus this relationship is injective if equal amounts and so we are able to take the same amount of living labor and mold it into hardened, material labor I.E. living labor ought to possess command of material labor, but not the other way around. But curiously this exact contradiction happens in the real market.

Rather, it is true that the definite quantity of labor in an object commands a greater quantity of living labor. Smith writes that the relationship between labor time, capital may not be consistent due to a conflating of the wrong definition (that quantity of labor and value of labor are the same, which they are not). He instead, author argues, should've went for the interpretation that there is a material difference between labor time and labor power.

### PT 2

Smith states that during primitive accumulation, the laborers' commodities are determined by labor time in that they determine exchange-value between other commodities of similar labor time. But when the accumulation of land and stock has been placed in the hands of the few, this is the direct precondition for capitalist production.

Profits, then, from this system specifically come from not the labor that the worker sells for his sustenance but directly unpaid labor, i.e. the proportion of labor that the producer has not paid for but is still receiving a profit regardless, in simple terms, uneven exchange. This latter part is the only factor responsible for surplus value. However, Smith unfortunately cannot distinguish between the general category of labour and surplus value, the source of most of his further mistakes.

Surplus value from the interest on capital can also come in two ways: either if this is through rent then this is borne out of the land implies that it came from productive labor, or that it is lending money (then this would only be a "secondary form of surplus value").

### PT 3

Smith believes general social labor acts as a superset that includes all possibilities from where surplus value can arise.

### PT 4

We first need to start with defining wages and money not as a commodity, but as direct materializations of general labor time. Only then we can see that the worker, after receiving his share, goes into the market interacting with the chain $M-C \rightarrow$ implies that there is a clear cut **max** when it comes to how much money/labor time this worker can spend. However, when the worker works he interacts with the chain $C - M'$, during this process he steadily imbues more labour time, which inturn creates more value than the sum of his wages, effectively making more labour out of thin air and underscoring the fundamentally imbalanced relationship between $M-C$ and $C-M'$.

### PT 5

Smith mistakes the capitalist's profit with the generated surplus value, which leads to a whole host of contradictions. For one, we know that profit is defined as the difference between the total capital put forth (made up of two distinct sectors as Smith puts it: the capital transferred over in the form of reusable technological devices PLUS the unpaid labor of the worker) and the original value of the commodity. If this were truly the case, then let us think about a simple thought experiment:

Say that there exist two identical factories in both the value of wages, same amount of laboring hours, and the rate of profit of both the factories are the same. The only characteristic in which they differ are the value of the constant capital employed by the factory, where the first factory's raw materials cost one thousand times more than the cost of the second factory. If we were to assume that Smith's proposition is true, then we would expect the value of the labor power to be different (the factory where raw materials cost more ought to have more value per one hour of labor). But empirically, this is not the case: it is rather that they receive the same amount of wages, but this shouldn't be the case according to Smith.

### PT 6

Capital is a productive value, but only as a relation, a force. It is not a source of value. It can only spur on the development of surplus labour, it cannot independently create new value, it exists only within materialised labour time. Profit, wages, all of them are borne out of this one productive force.

# Chapter 4 (Only Smith):

### PT 1 + PT 2

Smith correctly identifies the distinction between productive and unproductive labor. Productive labor can be defined as wage labor that has the capacity to generate surplus value as well as reproduce the amount of labour needed to justify one's wages. Productivity in the capitalist sense is based on the relative amount of value that the labour power can produce (if labor consumes more than it produces, then even though it undergoes the process of reproduction of capital, it is not productive to the capitalist.)

This line of reasoning follows closely from the Physiocrats. They posited that specifically agricultural labor could create value, but they had a misconception about value and related it to use values, not SNLT.

### PT 3

Author concedes a good point here attributed to Smith. From the standpoint of capitalist production, productive labor is wage labor that can create capital, it has no fetishistic properties, it exists simply as a commodity to the capitalist. Because we have previously defined capital as an relation between the exchanges of values, some function that takes in labor and outputs more labor time, productive labor is only productive insofar as it directly can be exchanged for capital, to partake in this circuit.

The purposes/reasons of one's labor does not define productive/unproductive labor. Cooks and waiters in hotels are productive laborers, as their labor is transformed into capital given towards the hotel, producing a surplus. But, workers who's labor is directly exchanged for revenue does not undergo this social relation, social transformation, and thus this labor is not "productive" in the capitalist sense. For example, a workman hired by a piano maker is a productive laborer because the surplus within the commodity that he helps produce, the finished piano, pays for both his sustenance as well as the profit of the firm (surplus). There is a real transformation here. But suppose that I simply hire a person to come to my house and build the piano for me. Then this would be unproductive labor because revenue is directly exchanged against labor, not the cycle.

The enlargening of the cycle is the important thing here. My wages do not go into the market when I give it to this piano fixer. What the piano fixer does with this money is superfluous, all that matters is that the revenue did not touch the circuit of capital and thus did not enlargen it. That alone is enough to classify it as unproductive labor.

### PT 4

Smith then notices the existence of a disparity in the forces of productive / unproductive labor. Because productive labor is the only type of labor that produces commodities according to Smith, and unproductive labor merely exchanges revenues for labor, one class will have their labor time amortized longer than the latter in the sense that their labor time will account to more influence within the aggregation of capital.

"There are only two forms of value... either pure labor power or the commodities themselves", being able to be freely exchanged so long as there exists the necessary constant capital. So there are merely distinctions of the same essence, the same "thing" that cannot be grasped at physically. The transformation process from labor to commodities must also be mystified for this reason.. this transformation is purely social, as it can take every form and be compatible with every commodity possible, thus it transcends the commodity's corporeal reality. This materialization of labor in the commodity must be remembered as a social one!

Because this materialization of labor is social, we must also consider the object of study i.e. the commodity produced as a result of this expenditure of labor power. If some worker created a machine with no value to anyone else but him with his own labor, say some machine that he will use merely for personal use, then this labor (even though it had the potential to be productive) is not actually productive insofar as it is a transfiguration that did not help magnify the power of capital.

Time is also an important factor in productive/unproductive labor. Because in unproductive labor labor cannot be stored anywhere, it has to be used right away! For example, we have aptly characterized personal services as a form of unproductive labor. Now, is there any way to store this potential usage of personal services for a long period of time? Say that you hired someone to fix your door. Can you input his labor that will allow him to fix your door and use it sometime else? Assuming you hired him for only one hour, you cannot freeze his potential for production in time and get that hour back, you must consume and exchange that labor immediately. But for a product, a commodity, you can always put it back into the cycle of capital to receive the amount of labor time embedded into this commodity, thus, it is able to be stored.

One of capitalism's contradictions then, is its handling of coagulated labor time. Because capital always wants to grow, the interests of sustaining and changing unproductive labor into productive labor is one of capitalism's main goals. An example: if I hire a worker to fix my broken door, then this is unproductive labor. But if the landlord hires the repairman to come fix the door, then suddenly this becomes productive labor, and so value is able to be stored. The landlord essentially stores that labor time in the building's value itself and slowly extracts it back from the tenants over the next ten years in the form of higher rent prices.
