# Background

**LTV** as a theory allows one to look at the amount of **labor** embedded in a commodity, and determine the relative **social value** of that commodity within a market (this statement presupposes that the labor embedded is useful).

This (in conjunction with the idea of constant/variable capital) has been historically useful to many socialists in the past because it allowed workers to quantify the amount of surplus value extracted from them by the capitalists, which in turn supported the idea that the proletariat ought to control the means of production, and eventually contributed to the rise of many socialists states around the world.

In recent years, with the rise of AI and, in turn, the likely outcome of mass unemployment through the existence and deployment of AGI in various different workplaces, many socialists and non-socialists have been grappling with the idea of LTV and its reliability. It seems that the main crux of this theoretical debate is that Marx and the Orthodox Marxists never accounted for the possibility of human labor becoming dispensable (and even unprofitable). This idea is aptly communicated in Capital with the notion of _"Abstract labor"_ and _"Socially necessary labor time"_ as uniquely **human** phenomena, which implies that a machine (or anything non-human) cannot create value.

I claim that this cannot be further from the truth, and is directly antithetical to Marxian economics. I argue that LTV as a framework is still incredibly useful in today's multipolar world if you are a socialist and it can be effectively utilized to predict and describe the post-AGI world.

Variable capital refers to human labor for now, but it can essentially be thought of as capital that can "imbue" its essence into individually non-productive units of capital, referred to as "constant capital" (think factory machines, steam engines, non-intelligent computers). During this $C$ stage, the labor of the variable capital emboldens and transforms the contstant capital and makes the commodity have **value** (we will elaborate on this a little later). Then the commodity is sold on the market for a marked up price, denoted by $M'$.
